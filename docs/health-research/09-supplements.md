# Supplements Research Document

> **Purpose**: Comprehensive knowledge base for yeww AI on evidence-based supplement guidance, separating hype from reality.

---

## The Supplement Industry Reality

### The Problem

- **$50+ billion industry** with strong financial incentives
- **Minimal regulation**: FDA doesn't approve supplements before sale
- **Quality varies wildly**: What's on label may not be in bottle
- **Marketing over science**: Claims often outpace evidence
- **Survivorship bias**: You hear success stories, not failures

### The Evidence Hierarchy

Most supplement claims come from:
1. Mechanistic reasoning ("X affects Y pathway")
2. Animal studies (mice aren't humans)
3. Observational studies (correlation ≠ causation)
4. Short-term, small human trials

What we actually need:
- Large, long-term randomized controlled trials
- Replication across multiple studies
- Meaningful clinical endpoints (not just biomarkers)

**Most supplements have NOT passed this bar.**

## Supplements With Good Evidence

### Tier 1: Strong Evidence

**Vitamin D** (if deficient)
- **Evidence**: Robust for bone health, immune function
- **Who needs it**: Those with deficiency (common in northern latitudes, dark skin, little sun exposure)
- **Dose**: Typically 1,000-4,000 IU/day depending on levels
- **Test first**: Get blood levels checked (aim for 30-50 ng/mL)
- **Note**: Taking more if not deficient likely has no benefit

**Creatine Monohydrate**
- **Evidence**: One of most studied supplements, good evidence
- **Benefits**: Muscle strength, power, and possibly cognitive function
- **Dose**: 3-5g/day (no need to load)
- **Safety**: Extensively studied, safe for healthy individuals
- **Note**: May cause slight water retention initially

**Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)**
- **Evidence**: Modest cardiovascular and anti-inflammatory benefits
- **Who benefits most**: Those who don't eat fatty fish regularly
- **Dose**: 1-3g EPA+DHA combined
- **Note**: Food sources (salmon, mackerel) may be superior
- **Quality matters**: Fish oil can oxidize; choose reputable brands

**Protein Powder** (if needed)
- **Evidence**: Protein itself has strong evidence; powder is just convenient delivery
- **Use case**: When whole food protein is inconvenient
- **Types**: Whey (fast), casein (slow), plant-based (adequate)
- **Note**: Not magic—same protein from food works identically

### Tier 2: Reasonable Evidence

**Magnesium**
- **Evidence**: Many people are deficient; helps sleep, muscle function
- **Forms**: Glycinate (best absorbed, calming), citrate (good absorption)
- **Dose**: 200-400mg/day
- **Note**: Get from food if possible (nuts, seeds, dark chocolate)

**Caffeine** (pre-workout)
- **Evidence**: Strong for acute performance enhancement
- **Dose**: 3-6mg/kg body weight
- **Timing**: 30-60 minutes before exercise
- **Caveat**: Tolerance develops; affects sleep; not for everyone

**Melatonin** (for sleep timing)
- **Evidence**: Good for jet lag and sleep timing issues
- **Dose**: 0.5-3mg (lower often better than higher)
- **Timing**: 30-60 minutes before desired sleep
- **Note**: Not a sleeping pill; helps with timing, not sleep depth

## Supplements With Weak/Mixed Evidence

### The "Promising in Mice" Category

**NAD+ Precursors (NMN, NR)**
- **Claim**: Anti-aging, energy metabolism
- **Reality**: Strong rodent data, human evidence weak and inconsistent
- **Cost**: Expensive
- **Verdict**: Interesting research, premature to recommend

**Resveratrol**
- **Claim**: Longevity, cardiovascular health
- **Reality**: Failed in most human trials despite initial hype
- **Verdict**: Likely ineffective at achievable doses

**Berberine**
- **Claim**: Blood sugar control, "nature's metformin"
- **Reality**: Some evidence for glucose, but also may blunt exercise benefits
- **Verdict**: Mixed; talk to doctor if diabetic/prediabetic

### The "Sounds Good But..." Category

**Multivitamins**
- **Claim**: Insurance against deficiencies
- **Reality**: Large trials show no mortality or disease benefit in well-nourished populations
- **Exception**: May help those with poor diets, absorption issues, or specific needs
- **Verdict**: Probably not harmful, probably not helpful for most

**Antioxidant Megadoses (Vitamin C, E, etc.)**
- **Claim**: Fight oxidative stress, prevent disease
- **Reality**: May actually interfere with exercise adaptation; some trials show harm
- **Verdict**: Get antioxidants from food, not pills

**BCAAs (Branched-Chain Amino Acids)**
- **Claim**: Muscle building, recovery
- **Reality**: If you eat adequate protein, BCAAs add nothing
- **Verdict**: Unnecessary if protein intake is sufficient

**Testosterone Boosters (Natural)**
- **Claim**: Increase testosterone naturally
- **Reality**: Most ingredients show minimal or no effect
- **Verdict**: Mostly marketing; lifestyle factors matter more

### The "Why Are People Taking This?" Category

**Collagen**
- **Claim**: Skin health, joint support
- **Reality**: Mixed evidence; body breaks it down to amino acids like any protein
- **Verdict**: May help joints, skin benefits questionable; just eat protein

**Apple Cider Vinegar**
- **Claim**: Weight loss, blood sugar, digestion
- **Reality**: Minimal effects at best; can damage teeth
- **Verdict**: Not worth it

**Detox/Cleanse Products**
- **Claim**: Remove toxins
- **Reality**: Your liver and kidneys do this; "detox" is not a medical concept
- **Verdict**: Avoid

## Special Categories

### Adaptogens

**What they are:** Herbs claimed to help body adapt to stress.

**Ashwagandha**
- Some evidence for stress reduction and sleep
- Dose: 300-600mg/day of root extract
- Relatively well-studied among adaptogens
- May interact with thyroid medications

**Rhodiola rosea**
- Some evidence for fatigue reduction
- Less research than ashwagandha
- May help with stress resilience

**Verdict:** Among the more plausible supplements, but evidence still modest.

### Pre-Workout Supplements

**What works:**
- Caffeine (strong evidence)
- Creatine (take daily, not just pre-workout)
- Beta-alanine (modest evidence for endurance; causes tingling)

**What's questionable:**
- Most proprietary blends
- Nitric oxide boosters (mixed evidence)
- Overhyped stimulant combinations

**Verdict:** Black coffee + creatine is cheaper and probably as effective as most pre-workouts.

### Sleep Supplements

**Reasonable evidence:**
- Melatonin (for timing)
- Magnesium glycinate (especially if deficient)
- Glycine (some evidence for sleep quality)

**Weak evidence:**
- Valerian root
- L-theanine (may help relaxation but sleep evidence weak)
- CBD (mixed evidence, quality control issues)

**Verdict:** Address sleep hygiene first; these are band-aids.

## Supplement Quality and Safety

### Quality Concerns

- **Label accuracy**: Studies find many products don't match labels
- **Contamination**: Heavy metals, undeclared drugs, banned substances
- **Third-party testing**: Look for NSF Certified for Sport, Informed Sport, USP verified

### How to Choose Brands

Look for:
- Third-party testing certification
- Transparent ingredient lists
- Established companies with reputation to protect
- cGMP (Current Good Manufacturing Practice) certified facilities

Avoid:
- Proprietary blends (hide actual doses)
- Outrageous claims
- Amazon-only brands with no reputation
- Prices too good to be true

### Drug Interactions

Many supplements interact with medications:
- St. John's Wort (many interactions)
- Fish oil (blood thinners)
- Vitamin K (warfarin)
- Magnesium (antibiotics, bisphosphonates)

**Always disclose supplements to healthcare providers.**

## The Philosophy: Food First

### Why Whole Foods Are Usually Better

1. **Synergy**: Nutrients work together in food
2. **Absorption**: Often better from food matrix
3. **Safety**: Harder to overdose on food
4. **Additional compounds**: Fiber, phytonutrients, etc.
5. **Satiety**: Food fills you up; pills don't

### When Supplements Make Sense

- **True deficiency**: Confirmed by blood test
- **Restricted diet**: Vegan (B12), allergies, etc.
- **Increased needs**: Athletes, pregnancy, age-related absorption issues
- **Convenience**: Protein powder when whole food impractical
- **Specific conditions**: Medical recommendation

### The Hierarchy

1. Get your diet right first (covers 90%+)
2. Address lifestyle factors (sleep, exercise, stress)
3. Test for deficiencies before supplementing
4. Use evidence-based supplements if needed
5. Ignore 95% of supplement marketing

## Actionable Insights for yeww

### When to Surface This Knowledge

**Good triggers:**
- User asks about supplements
- User mentions specific supplement by name
- User trying to optimize with supplements before basics
- User overwhelmed by supplement marketing
- User asks what's worth taking

**Bad triggers:**
- User has medical condition requiring specific supplementation
- User needs pharmaceutical advice

### Example Conversations

**User asks what supplements to take:**
> "Honest answer: most supplements aren't worth the money. The exceptions with good evidence are vitamin D (if deficient), creatine, and omega-3s (if you don't eat fish). Beyond that, you're probably better off spending the money on quality food and sleep."

**User mentions trendy supplement:**
> "The hype usually outpaces the evidence. [X supplement] has some interesting research, but mostly in mice or small studies. The fundamentals—sleep, nutrition, exercise—will do more than any supplement. If the basics are dialed, it's not harmful to experiment, but keep expectations realistic."

**User taking many supplements:**
> "Worth asking: are the basics handled? No supplement stack replaces good sleep, nutrition, and exercise. I'd focus there first. For what you're taking—a few have decent evidence, but some of those are probably expensive placebo."

**User asks about pre-workout:**
> "Most pre-workouts are expensive caffeine with marketing. Black coffee and creatine (taken daily) covers most of what actually works. The tingly feeling from beta-alanine isn't enhancement, just a sensation. Save your money unless you really prefer the convenience."

### Phrasing Guidelines

**Do say:**
- "Most supplements aren't worth the money"
- "Evidence in humans is what matters"
- "Food first, supplements for gaps"
- "A few things have good evidence, most don't"

**Don't say:**
- "You should take X" (individual variation, medical considerations)
- "Supplements are useless" (some have evidence)
- "X is dangerous" (unless truly contraindicated)
- Specific dosing for individuals (too personal)

## Summary

**The supplement reality check:**

1. **Most supplements don't work** as advertised
2. **A few have good evidence**: Vitamin D (if deficient), creatine, omega-3s
3. **Quality varies wildly**: Third-party testing matters
4. **Marketing exceeds evidence**: Be skeptical of claims
5. **Food first**: Supplements can't replace a good diet
6. **Basics beat biohacks**: Sleep, exercise, nutrition > any pill

The unsexy truth: money spent on quality food, good sleep, and consistent exercise will outperform almost any supplement stack.
